# VitalLog Lab Reference Glossary

Plain-language explanations of common lab test terms, written for VitalLog to
ground its explanations with citeable references.

These definitions are original text written for this project. The underlying
facts are based on public-domain consumer-health information from the U.S.
National Library of Medicine (MedlinePlus / NIH). Reference ranges are
approximate and **vary by laboratory, age, sex, and method** — always read the
range printed on your own report.

This is general information, not medical advice. Only a licensed professional
can interpret results for your specific situation.

## Hemoglobin (Hb)
Hemoglobin is the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen from the lungs
to the rest of the body. A hemoglobin test is part of a complete blood count
(CBC). Typical adult ranges are roughly 13.5–17.5 g/dL for men and 12.0–15.5
g/dL for women. A low value may be described as anemia; a high value can have
many causes. The exact reference range is printed on your report.

## Hematocrit (Hct)
Hematocrit is the percentage of your blood volume made up of red blood cells. It
is closely related to hemoglobin and is also part of a complete blood count.
Typical adult ranges are about 38.3–48.6% for men and 35.5–44.9% for women. Low
or high values are interpreted alongside hemoglobin and other results.

## White Blood Cells (WBC)
White blood cells are part of the immune system and help the body fight
infection. The WBC count is part of a complete blood count. A typical adult
range is roughly 4.0–11.0 x10^9/L (4,000–11,000 cells per microliter). Higher
counts can occur with infection or inflammation; lower counts have many possible
causes. Interpretation depends on the full clinical picture.

## Platelets (PLT)
Platelets are small cell fragments that help blood clot and stop bleeding. The
platelet count is part of a complete blood count. A typical adult range is about
150–400 x10^9/L. Counts outside this range can affect bleeding or clotting and
are interpreted by a clinician alongside other findings.

## LDL Cholesterol (LDL-C)
LDL is often called "bad" cholesterol because higher levels are associated with
a greater risk of plaque buildup in arteries over time. It is measured as part
of a lipid panel. General guidance often treats an LDL below about 100 mg/dL as
optimal, but target levels depend on a person's overall cardiovascular risk and
should be set with a clinician.

## HDL Cholesterol (HDL-C)
HDL is often called "good" cholesterol because it helps remove other cholesterol
from the bloodstream. It is measured as part of a lipid panel. For HDL, higher
is generally considered better; values below about 40 mg/dL (men) or 50 mg/dL
(women) are often flagged as low. Interpretation is individualized.

## Triglycerides
Triglycerides are a type of fat (lipid) in the blood that the body uses for
energy. They are measured as part of a lipid panel, usually after fasting. A
level below about 150 mg/dL is commonly described as normal. Higher levels can
be influenced by diet, alcohol, weight, and other conditions.

## Fasting Glucose
Fasting glucose measures the amount of sugar in the blood after not eating for
several hours. It is used to screen for and monitor diabetes. A typical fasting
range is about 70–99 mg/dL; 100–125 mg/dL is often labeled prediabetes, and
126 mg/dL or higher on more than one test may indicate diabetes. Diagnosis is
made by a clinician, not by a single number.

## Hemoglobin A1c (HbA1c)
Hemoglobin A1c reflects your average blood sugar over roughly the past two to
three months. It is reported as a percentage. Below 5.7% is generally considered
normal, 5.7%–6.4% is often labeled prediabetes, and 6.5% or higher may indicate
diabetes. As with all results, only a clinician can interpret it for you.

## Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone (TSH)
TSH is a hormone from the pituitary gland that signals the thyroid to make
thyroid hormones. It is used to check how well the thyroid is working. A common
reference range is about 0.4–4.0 mIU/L, though ranges vary by lab and situation.
High or low TSH can point in different directions and is interpreted with other
thyroid tests.

## Creatinine
Creatinine is a waste product from normal muscle activity that the kidneys
filter out of the blood. A blood creatinine test helps assess kidney function.
Typical adult ranges are roughly 0.7–1.3 mg/dL for men and 0.6–1.1 mg/dL for
women, depending on muscle mass and lab method. It is often interpreted together
with an estimated filtration rate (eGFR).

## ALT and AST (Liver Enzymes)
ALT (alanine aminotransferase) and AST (aspartate aminotransferase) are enzymes
found mainly in the liver. They are measured in a liver panel, and higher levels
can be a sign that liver cells are stressed or damaged. Typical ranges are often
around 7–56 U/L for ALT and 10–40 U/L for AST, but ranges vary. Results are
interpreted in context by a clinician.
